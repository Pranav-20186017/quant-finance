Justification for econmic indicators chosen to correlate with the US GCF Repo Index

  • 3-Month Treasury Bill Rate (DGS3MO)
  • Explanation: This rate represents the return on an investment in a short-term U.S. government debt instrument (a Treasury bill) that matures in 3 months. It's a reliable indicator of short-term interest rates in the U.S.
  • Rationale for Selection: The GCF Repo Index reflects the cost of overnight borrowing collateralized by Treasury and agency debt, which are closely related to short-term U.S. government debt. Comparing the GCF Repo Index with DGS3MO allows for insights into how short-term government interest rates might be influencing the repo market.
  • Federal Funds Rate (FEDFUNDS)
  • Explanation: As mentioned previously, the federal funds rate is the interest rate at which depository institutions lend reserve balances to other depository institutions overnight. It's central to U.S. monetary policy.
  • Rationale for Selection: The GCF Repo Index and the federal funds rate both represent overnight borrowing rates, but they differ in their collateral requirements and counterparty risks. Comparing the two can provide insights into how shifts in monetary policy or interbank lending conditions might be impacting the secured lending market.
  • TED Spread (TEDRATE)
  • Explanation: The TED spread measures the difference between the interest rates on interbank loans (as represented by the 3-month LIBOR) and short-term U.S. government debt (3-month Treasury bills). It's a common gauge of credit risk in the broader economy.
  • Rationale for Selection: A widening TED spread indicates increasing perceived credit risk in the banking system, which could influence the demand for secured borrowing (like repos). By analyzing the relationship between the TED spread and the GCF Repo Index, we can gain insights into how systemic credit risk perceptions might be influencing repo rates.

  • 10-Year Treasury Constant Maturity Rate (GS10):
  • Explanation: This is the yield on US Treasury securities at a constant maturity of 10 years. It is a key indicator reflecting long-term interest rate expectations and is often used as a benchmark for other interest rates, including mortgage rates and bank loan rates.
  • Rationale: Changes in the 10-year Treasury yield can influence the pricing and demand for other fixed income securities. As yields rise, newly issued bonds become more attractive than existing bonds with lower yields, impacting trading volumes.
  • Effective Federal Funds Rate (FEDFUNDS):
  • Explanation: This is the interest rate at which depository institutions trade federal funds (balances held at Federal Reserve Banks) with each other overnight. It is the main tool the Federal Reserve uses to influence monetary conditions and thereby affect economic activity.
  • Rationale: The Federal Funds Rate is a short-term rate, and its movements can influence the entire yield curve. When the Federal Reserve changes its target for the Federal Funds Rate, it can affect trading volumes across various maturities in the fixed income market.
  • Spread between 10-Year and 2-Year Treasury Constant Maturity (T10Y2Y):
  • Explanation: This metric provides the yield curve spread between the 10-Year and 2-Year Treasury securities. It's an indicator of the shape of the yield curve and can hint at future economic conditions.
  • Rationale: The yield curve, specifically the spread between long-term and short-term rates, offers insights into market expectations about future economic growth and inflation.
